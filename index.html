In addition to serving as an SGA Senator, I am a member of the UMBC chapter of the National Society of Black Engineers. I have also led as a Team Captain in UMBC’s Relay for Life event.

UMBC SGA Initiative Tracker

Team Members: Justin Chavez, Matthew Landen, Joshua Massey, Darren Stephens

The UMBC Student Government Association is a large student-run organization that advocates for and develops projects that are of interest to the undergraduate population of UMBC. An challenge that the SGA faces is internal accountability, both within and outside the organization. For years, the status of any current initiatives have been based on hearsay and speculation within the organization. In addition to this, outside of SGA, many undergraduates at UMBC were not aware of what their elected SGA officers and general members do in their positions. In order to increase the accountability of the organization, my team and I began the development of an SGA Initiative Tracker. With this, the entire UMBC community will be able to see exactly what each branch, department, and member of SGA is working on, therefore increasing the awareness and accountability of the organization along with its the reputation with University administration and staff. This projects links a public website with a mySQL database using PHP and HTML. The project began as a hackUMBC Fall 2014 project and is currently under development.

UMBC Robotics on the Surface: Wall-E Robot

Team Members: Kenneth Amos, Jacob Bass, Joshua Massey, Nathaniel Scott

The main task of the UMBC Robotics on the Surface (UROS) project is to design, build, and test a VEX robotic vehicle that will compete in a 10′ × 10′ arena. Working in a pre-assigned team, we used their robotic vehicle in a four-minute competition to earn the maximum number points possible while completing simulated real-world missions: an urban rescue — save men from a "burning" building; an oil spill clean-up — remove oil from fish pond; bomb disposal — diffuse a bomb; and disaster relief — remove a boulder from the path. This project was a part of the Introduction to Engineering (ENES 101) course, a course for engineering majors that encompasses topics from each of the engineering majors at UMBC.

True Grit: A College Semester Simulator

Team Members: Loryn Chen, Anthony Everett, Joshua Massey, Christopher Roerty, Jeremy Streb

Also called "True Grit, the Semester Game," True Grit is a game that simulates a semester in college, effectively measuring the user’s mental stamina and toughness–or "True Grit"–when faced with the challenges of college life. It takes in values on how one spends time every week — work hours, hours in class, engagement in class, study hours, hours in study groups, academic resource hours, hours spent taking care of oneself, hours of solitary leisure, and hours of leisure with others. The game then calculates three outcomes based on preset algorithms — the user’s levels of happiness and grades (percentages out of 100) as well as the amount of money earned that week. Added features include a random event generator, which randomly selects an event from a list of 40 possible choices that effect the outcomes after calculations. The game also makes use of animated GIFs in addition to sliders and buttons from the ControlP5 library. The program is written in Processing, an open source programming language based on Java that generates visual output.

Real Time Global Illumination Solutions to the Radiosity Algorithm Using Hybrid CPU/GPU Nodes

UMBC REU Site: Interdisciplinary Program in High Performance Computing
Team Members: Oluwapelumi Adenikinju, Julian Gilyard, Joshua Massey, Thomas Stitt

"In computer graphics, an integral portion of the rendering timeline is solving for the light distribution of a scene. When realism is important, a solution involving global illumination – allowing light rays to bounce before hitting the viewer – is desired. One method that awards particularly good results for diffuse surfaces is the radiosity algorithm, despite the fact that this method is computationally expensive – using it for near-real to real time rendering is not generally practical. Starting with the existing computational radiosity solver rrv, we determine how runtimes can be reduced through a combination of multi-core CPU to massively-parallel GPU architectures available in the cluster maya of the UMBC High Performance Computing Facility."
